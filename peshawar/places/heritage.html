City Museum, Gorkhatri


City Museum, Gorkhatriis an archaeology museum located on the archaeological site of Gorkhatri, Peshawar in the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province of Pakistan. The museum was inaugurated on 23 March 2006 body Akram Khan Durrani, the then Chief Minister of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa

The museum consists of three galleries: archaeological, ethnological, and antiquities. The latter is the most recent addition, exhibiting the antiquities of the British Raj which provide detailed information about archaeological and ethnological profile of Peshawar.

Overview of History

Gorkhatri is the highest place in Peshawar and it is located at the heart of walled city of old Peshawar. The site having a huge deposit of the historical periods began from the Indo-Greeksor even earlier. The current structure of the Gorkhatri complex was established by Jahanara Begum, the daughter of the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in 1640 A.D

Peshawar Museum

Peshawar MuseumThe Peshawar Museum (Urdu: پشاور میوزیم(colloquial); پشاور عجائب گھر (official)) is a museum located in Peshawar, capital of Pakistan's Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province. The museum houses a collection of Buddhist artwork from the ancient Gandhara region.

Overview of History

The Peshawar Museum was founded in 1907 as "Victoria Hall," in memory of Queen Victoria The two-story building was built in a syncretic architectural style consisting of British, Hindu, Buddhist and Mughal Islamic styles.

Initially, the museum had only one exhibition hall, but two more were added in 1969–70. In 2004–05, the museum was further expanded with the construction of a new block with two galleries, two halls for the museum's collection in storage, offices for the provincial directorate of archaeology, a conservation laboratory and a cafeteria. The historic exhibition hall was also renovated at that time.

Sethi Haveli

Sethi Haveli, also known as Sethi House, is a beautiful example of 19th-century architecture in Sethi Mohallah, Peshawar. Built between 1835 and 1884 by the wealthy Sethi trading family, the mansion blends Mughal, Central Asian, and local architectural styles.

Historical Background

The Sethi family, originally Hindu Khatris from Jhelum, moved to Peshawar in the early 1800s. Successful traders, they built seven havelis in the city. Their business flourished across Central Asia until the Russian Revolution of 1917 disrupted their trade routes, leading to financial decline.

Architectural Features

The mansion is made of brick, timber, and lime plaster, with intricate wooden carvings, stained glass windows, and elaborate frescoes. Key features include tehkhanas (underground basements), dalaans (large halls), balakhanas (upper floors), a central courtyard with a marble fountain, and decorative chinikhanas (small display niches).

Design & Layout

Inspired by Bukhara’s vernacular architecture, the haveli includes 12 rooms, four basements, colourful glass ceilings, carved wooden doors, and ventilated atriums. Highlights include the Takht-e-Sulaimani — a wooden resting spot for sunbathing in winter — and mirror-adorned ceilings.

Visiting Information

Located near Ghanta Ghar and Qissa Khwani Bazaar, Sethi House is open daily from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM. Guided tours are available, offering insights into the region’s cultural and architectural history.

Sethi Mohallah

Sethi Mohallah is a historic neighbourhood famed for its havelis built by the influential Sethi family. The homes reflect a mix of Central Asian, Persian, and South Asian designs, with wooden carvings, frescoes, and airy courtyards. Tekhanas were cleverly designed to keep homes cool during Peshawar’s hot summers.

Cultural Legacy

The Sethis were respected traders and philanthropists in Peshawar, contributing to public projects like wells and mosques. The most famous mansion — Sethi House — was converted into a museum in 2010, preserving its rich architectural heritage for future generations.
